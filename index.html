PPP Dimension Selector Self-check

Use this quick self-check to identify which PPP dimensions might be most useful to focus on. Rate each statement on a scale from 1–5:

  • 1 = Strongly disagree
  • 2 = Disagree
  • 3 = Neutral
  • 4 = Agree
  • 5 = Strongly agree

Lower scores often indicate areas where additional focus may be helpful. This tool is for your own reflection and is not stored or submitted.

The self-check has 36 statements and usually takes around 5–7 minutes to complete.

In my teaching, students regularly participate in activities (for example, discussion, polling, group work) rather than mainly listening to me talk.
I deliberately design activities so that quieter or marginalised students can participate in ways that feel safe.
During teaching, I monitor who is participating and adjust my strategies so a wide range of student voices are heard.
At the start of a topic, I explore what students already know or believe about the content.
When introducing new concepts I explicitly connect them to students’ prior knowledge, experiences or professional contexts.
I treat student misconceptions as learning opportunities and design explanations or activities that help them reconsider their thinking.
My learning materials and examples reflect a diversity of cultures, identities and perspectives, including Indigenous perspectives where appropriate.
I design activities and assessments that are accessible for students with different abilities, locations and circumstances.
I intentionally build a class culture where all students are respected, feel safe to contribute and can see themselves as belonging.
I design learning tasks that require students to analyse, evaluate or create, not just recall information.
In class, I use questions that prompt students to explain their reasoning or compare different viewpoints.
I give students opportunities to apply concepts to unfamiliar or complex problems.
For each course or session, I clearly communicate the intended learning outcomes to students.
I use rubrics, examples or co-created criteria so that students know what quality work looks like.
I help students see how current learning connects to later courses, professional practice or graduate attributes.
I regularly connect theoretical ideas to real-world cases, scenarios or workplace examples.
I design learning activities where students apply knowledge to authentic, messy or interdisciplinary problems.
Where relevant, I invite perspectives or content from other disciplines to deepen understanding.
I make purposeful choices about when to use online, face-to-face or hybrid modes to support learning.
Students use digital tools (for example, the LMS, shared documents or discussion boards) to collaborate and participate.
I review how well my teaching spaces (physical or digital) work for different students and adjust my design accordingly.
I provide a clear course or module-level roadmap so students can see how learning is organised over time.
Within a class or online module, activities and resources follow a logical sequence that builds understanding.
I signpost key transitions (for example, why we are doing this next) and keep instructions concise and easy to follow.
I design low-stakes activities (quizzes, draft submissions, polls, reflections) so that students and I can gauge learning progress.
I explicitly build time for students to respond to feedback (for example, revising work or setting improvement goals).
Feedback in my course focuses on what students did well, what needs work, and specific next steps.
Some learning activities in my course invite students to be creative, experimental or imaginative in how they respond.
I pay attention to student workload and wellbeing and talk explicitly about strategies for managing study demands.
I encourage students to see how skills from this course will be useful beyond assessment and into their longer-term futures.
I explicitly teach or model study and learning strategies rather than assuming students already have them.
Students are given chances to plan their learning (for example, setting goals or choosing approaches) and to review how those plans worked.
I use self- or peer-assessment activities that help students judge the quality of their own work.
I discuss with students how to evaluate the credibility of digital sources and information.
When AI tools are relevant, I explicitly address ethical and responsible use with students.
I design at least some tasks that require students to use digital or AI tools critically rather than uncritically accepting outputs.
