PPP Dimension Selector Self‑check

Use this quick self‑check to identify which PPP dimensions might be most useful to focus on. Rate each statement on a scale from 1–5:

  • 1 = Strongly disagree
  • 2 = Disagree
  • 3 = Neutral / unsure
  • 4 = Agree
  • 5 = Strongly agree

Lower scores often indicate areas where additional focus may be helpful. This tool is for your own reflection and is not stored or submitted. It should take about 5–7 minutes to complete.

In my teaching, students regularly participate in activities (for example, discussion, polling, group work) rather than mainly listening to me talk.

I rarely design activities so that quieter or marginalised students can participate in ways that feel safe.

During teaching, I monitor who is participating and adjust my strategies to ensure a wide range of student voices are heard.

At the start of a topic, I explore what students already know or believe about the content.

I rarely connect new concepts to students’ prior knowledge, experiences or professional contexts.

I deliberately surface common misconceptions and design explanations or activities that help students rethink their thinking.

My learning materials and examples reflect a diversity of cultures, identities and perspectives, including Indigenous perspectives where appropriate.

I rarely review my course through an equity or accessibility lens to identify barriers that some students may face.

I intentionally build a class culture where all students are respected, feel safe to contribute and can see themselves as belonging.

I design tasks that require students to analyse, evaluate or create, not just recall information.

In class, I seldom ask students to explain their reasoning or compare different viewpoints.

I give students opportunities to apply concepts to unfamiliar or complex problems.

For each course or session, I clearly communicate the intended learning outcomes to students.

I rarely use rubrics, examples or co‑created criteria to make expectations for quality work explicit.

I help students see how current learning connects to later courses, professional practice or graduate attributes.

I regularly connect theoretical ideas to real‑world cases, scenarios or workplace examples.

I seldom invite students to apply knowledge to authentic, messy or interdisciplinary problems.

When possible, I invite perspectives or content from other disciplines to deepen understanding.

I make purposeful choices about when to use online, face‑to‑face or hybrid modes to support learning.

Students rarely use digital tools (for example, the LMS, shared documents or discussion boards) to collaborate and participate.

I review how well my teaching spaces (physical or digital) work for different students and adjust my design accordingly.

I provide a clear weekly or module‑level roadmap so students can see how learning is organised over time.

Within a class or online module, activities and resources rarely follow a logical sequence that builds understanding.

I signpost key transitions (for example, why we are doing this next) and keep instructions concise and easy to follow.

I design low‑stakes activities (quizzes, draft submissions, polls, reflections) primarily so that students and I can gauge learning progress.

I seldom build in time for students to respond to feedback (for example, revising work or setting improvement goals).

I use self‑ or peer‑assessment activities that help students judge the quality of their own work.

Some learning activities in my course invite students to be creative, experimental or imaginative in how they respond.

I pay little attention to student workload and wellbeing when planning activities and rarely talk about strategies for managing study demands.

I encourage students to see how skills from this course will be useful beyond assessment and into their longer‑term futures.

When AI tools are relevant, I explicitly address ethical and responsible use with students.

I rarely talk with students about how to evaluate the credibility of digital sources and information.

I design at least some tasks that require students to use digital or AI tools critically rather than uncritically accepting outputs.
