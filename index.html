PPP Dimension Selector Self-check

Use this quick self-check to identify which PPP dimensions might be most useful to focus on first. Rate each statement on a scale from 1–5:

  • 1 = Strongly disagree
  • 2 = Disagree
  • 3 = Neutral / unsure
  • 4 = Agree
  • 5 = Strongly agree

Higher ratings mean the statement is more true of your current practice. Lower scores often highlight areas where additional focus could be helpful.

This self-check takes around 5–7 minutes. It is for your own reflection only and is not stored or submitted.

Rate each statement

Please respond to every statement below. Some statements are deliberately quite specific so that a rating of “5” is reserved for practices you do consistently and confidently.

Statement 1 2 3 4 5
In my teaching, most students take an active part in learning activities rather than mainly listening to me talk.
I routinely use participation strategies that give quieter or marginalised students low-risk ways to contribute.
I rarely rely on a single activity format (for example, whole-class discussion only) for an entire session.
Before introducing a new concept, I deliberately check what students already know or believe about the topic.
I explicitly invite students to connect ideas to their own professional, cultural or lived experiences.
I seldom move on from a key idea without first checking that students can explain it in their own words.
Course materials and examples in my teaching reflect a variety of cultures, identities and perspectives, including Indigenous ones where appropriate.
I design tasks with flexibility (for example, topic, format or mode) so that students with different needs can demonstrate their learning equitably.
I intentionally build classroom norms that help students feel respected, safe to contribute, and a sense of belonging.
I design activities that require students to analyse, compare or evaluate ideas rather than just recall information.
I routinely ask follow-up questions that push students to justify or extend their thinking.
Students regularly have opportunities to apply concepts to unfamiliar or complex problems.
For each course or session, I clearly communicate the intended learning outcomes to students.
Assessment criteria or rubrics are explicit and used with students to define what quality work looks like.
I regularly help students connect current learning to future courses, professional practice or graduate capabilities.
I link key theoretical ideas to real-world cases, scenarios or work examples.
Students have opportunities to use knowledge from other disciplines or subjects when working in my course.
I intentionally design tasks that require students to apply theory to practice rather than treating them as separate.
I make purposeful choices about when to use online, face-to-face or hybrid modes to support learning.
Students regularly use digital tools (for example, the LMS, shared documents or discussion boards) to support interaction and collaboration.
When teaching, I review how well my learning spaces (physical or digital) are working and adjust layout, tools or activities accordingly.
I provide a clear weekly or module-level roadmap so that students can see how learning is organised over time.
Within a class or online module, activities and resources follow a logical sequence that builds understanding.
I routinely signpost key transitions in a session (for example, wrapping up one idea before moving to the next).
Students receive feedback from me or peers early enough to influence their next attempt at a task.
I design low-stakes activities (quizzes, draft submissions, polls, reflections) primarily so that students and I can gauge learning progress.
I explicitly build in time for students to respond to feedback (for example, revising work or setting improvement goals).
Some learning activities in my course invite students to be creative, experimental or imaginative in how they respond.
I pay attention to student workload and wellbeing and talk explicitly about strategies for managing study demands.
I encourage students to see how skills from this course will be useful beyond assessment and into their longer-term futures.
I explicitly teach or model study and learning strategies rather than assuming students already have them.
Students are given chances to plan their learning (for example, setting goals or choosing approaches) and to review how those plans worked.
I use self- or peer-assessment activities that help students judge the quality of their own work.
I discuss with students how to evaluate the credibility of digital sources and information.
When AI tools are relevant, I explicitly address ethical and responsible use with students.
I design at least some tasks that require students to use digital or AI tools critically rather than uncritically accepting outputs.
